THE IMMIGRANT EXPERIENCE

The journey and arrival

1910. After a 16-day steamship journey from Naples, you arrive at Ellis Island. You undergo a rigorous physical exam looking for signs of contagious disease or debilitating condition. An immigration official interrogates you about your political affiliations and the amount of money you have with you. Satisfied that you don’t belong to any subversive groups and that the $15 in your pocket is sufficient to keep you from becoming a ward of the state, you are permitted to enter. So are most of the people on board with you. There will be no limit on the number of immigrants until 1921.

THE IMMIGRANT EXPERIENCE

A reunion delayed

1921. With an apartment in Little Italy and a steady job at the Brooklyn Navy Yard, you are ready to summon Lucia and your infant son, Roberto and make a life in America. But immigration law has changed. Under the Emergency Quota Act of 1921, each country gets a maximum number of immigrants. Visas are given on a first-come-first-served basis at the port of entry, and the number of Italians seeking entry far exceed the quota. If Lucia and Roberto make the passage, they might get turned away.

1922. Lucia and Roberto arrive in New York, but their ship is held in the harbor. The Italian quotas have already been filled for the month. The boat returns to Italy. THE IMMIGRANT EXPERIENCE

Funding your passage

You meet an agent of the Central Pacific company in San Francisco at a stall near the docks. He tells you about the credit-ticket system: a hiring agency will pay for your passage and you will sign up to a three-year contract earning $14 per month. You will repay the company in monthly installments until the cost of your passage is cleared. Your uncle warns you about the exploitative tactics used by devious agents, or chu chai tau (swineherds). Instead, he offers to cover the cost. But your uncle is not entirely trustworthy. He has charged your father an unjust interest rate on a loan, and he warns you that if you fail to repay him, the cost of your passage will be added to your father’s debt.

THE IMMIGRANT EXPERIENCE

Life under contract

1869. You are transported to Utah to work on the final stretch of the Transcontinental Railroad. You join the small army of laborers already there and those working on the Union Pacific line coming from the east. The latter group is mostly made up of white immigrants. Many see Chinese workers as a threat and violent incidents break out in the camps. The tent where you eat and gamble is burned, and a number of Chinese workers are killed. In an attempt to ease tensions, you are sent to harvest sugar cane and cotton on plantations in Louisiana. Your debt takes longer to work off than you were led to believe. In addition to repaying your advance and cost of passage, the company deducts money for your shabby accommodations and paltry food allocation. Still, you manage to save more than you would have in China.

1881. You have decided to bring Ling and your infant son Zhang Wei, born in your absence, to join you in California but you underestimate the difficulty of gaining admission. Violence and discrimination in Seattle

1886. The economy isn’t in great shape and the white population has grown increasingly hostile toward an easily identifiable group who will work for lower wages. Because of Wang Jie’s connections within the city’s white business community, Rivera & Sons emerges from the riots relatively unscathed, but your wife and children are afraid to go outside. Settled in San Francisco

Wang Jie recognizes your sacrifices and loyalty during your time in Seattle. You settle in Chinatown with your growing family, and your home life is very happy.
